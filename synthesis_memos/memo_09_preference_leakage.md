# Synthesis Memo 09 — Preference Leakage

**Paper**: Li et al., "Preference Leakage: A Contamination Problem in LLM-as-a-Judge," 2025  
**Date**: 2026-04-29  
**Author**: Kemeriya  
**Status**: Path B reading — directly informs judge rotation policy

---

## Core Claims

Li et al. define "preference leakage" as a contamination problem specific to RLHF pipelines: when the same LLM (or same model family) generates candidate outputs and judges their quality, the judge inflates scores for outputs that match its own generation style and vocabulary. This creates a circular feedback loop — the trained model increasingly resembles the judge's preferences, not objective quality. Li et al. show this effect is measurable across multiple model families and is not eliminated by chain-of-thought prompting or rubric conditioning alone. The primary mitigation is generator-judge independence: use different model families for each role.

---

## Where I Agree

The generator-judge independence recommendation is applied directly in generation_scripts/judge_rotation_log.jsonl: qwen/qwen3-235b-a22b generates synthesis task seeds; anthropic/claude-sonnet-4-6 judges them. These are different model families with different training lineages. The rotation is documented task-by-task so the leakage control is auditable.

For preference pair construction, the same principle applies: the "chosen" rewrites for tasks without a trace-derived correct output are generated by a dev-tier Qwen model; they are judged for rubric compliance by the scoring_evaluator.py deterministic rules rather than by an LLM. This sidesteps the leakage problem entirely for the most common case: the judge is a rule-based Python script, not a model.

---

## Where I Disagree

Li et al.'s threat model is specifically about **win-rate inflation in RLHF** — the trained model converges toward outputs that its generator-judge combination prefers, inflating win rates on benchmarks evaluated by the same judge family. Their experimental setup involves a generator model M, a judge model J (same or different family), and measures whether J gives M's outputs preferential treatment.

**The Tenacious-Bench preference leakage scenario is categorically different.** In Tenacious-Bench, the "generator" of rejected outputs is the Week 10 Conversion Engine (a multi-component LLM pipeline), not the model being trained. The "chosen" outputs are either: (a) derived deterministically from the task's ground_truth fields, or (b) generated by Qwen3-8B and then validated against the deterministic rubric. The LLM being trained (Qwen3-0.6B LoRA) never generates either chosen or rejected outputs during training data construction.

Li et al.'s contamination loop — model generates, model judges, model trains, model generates better, model judges higher — requires the same model to appear in all three stages. In Tenacious-Bench, the training model (Qwen3-0.6B) appears in none of the data construction stages. The leakage risk is structurally absent, not merely mitigated.

**Where leakage is a real risk in the Tenacious pipeline**: the tone_check LLM judge in scoring_evaluator.py uses Qwen3-8B (via OpenRouter) for tone scoring during dataset authoring. If the training model is Qwen3-0.6B and the tone judge is Qwen3-8B, they share a model family. If tone scores are used to filter or weight preference pairs, this is a genuine Li et al. leakage scenario. The mitigation already in place — tone check uses heuristic fallback during iteration, LLM judge reserved for sealed held-out evaluation with Claude Sonnet 4.6 — correctly breaks this loop.

---

## Implication for Tenacious-Bench

The judge rotation policy is correctly applied for synthesis task judging. The stronger insight from Li et al. is that the tone_check LLM judge should never be from the same family as the training model when scoring training-partition tasks. Since Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-0.6B share a family, any tone scoring used during preference pair construction should route through Claude or a non-Qwen model. This constraint is documented in methodology.md Section 4.1 (preference-leakage prevention) but should be made explicit in the training notebook as a guard before running the training loop.
